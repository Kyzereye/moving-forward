# Chapter 2: What Moving Forward Means

*Moving Forward: Stop Looking in the Rear View Mirror*

---

How many half-finished projects are in your garage, basement, bookshelf, or the corner of some room in your home?  It is about not moving forward. When we leave things half done, we leave a little of our attention and energy stuck there. These lost projects are not just clutter in the room, but also clutter in your mind.  We tell ourselves we will get back to them, but we don't. The reasons we stop are many, and they are not really the point. What matters is that most of the time we have moved on to something else, and these projects are now in the rearview mirror. We are not looking at what it could still become. We are looking back at what it was supposed to have been, and that keeps us from asking what we do next.

Moving forward is not about replaying old failures or staring at the life you once had. It is not waiting for the right conditions or the right version of yourself to show up. When you do that, you are looking back at a version of yourself that no longer exists. Moving forward means taking the next step, even a small one. It means putting your attention on what comes next rather than on what already happened or what you thought it should have been. You do not have to forget the past. You just have to stop living in it. You do not need the whole route figured out. You only need to stop looking in the rearview mirror and start looking at the road ahead. It takes more energy to get something moving from a standstill than to keep it moving once it has started. Projects that sit stale drain you and feel heavier the longer they sit. One step is enough to break that. Then momentum takes over.

I will give you two stories. One where I moved forward by finishing, and another where I moved forward by letting go.

The first is about woodworking. It was never just one project. It was a pattern. I would start something, get it partially done, and then a new idea would catch my attention. Something shinier. I went from cutting boards to planter boxes, from planter boxes to epoxy tables, and from epoxy tables back to "smalls that sell." Each new idea felt more exciting than the one I had left half done. I kept collecting wood, materials, and supplies for projects I had not finished yet and for projects I had not even started. The garage filled up. Every time I walked in, I could see the evidence of every project I had abandoned. Every time I pulled my car into the garage, I could see them in the rearview mirror as I backed in. Literally and figuratively, I was looking at my unfinished life every time I parked.

I would get frustrated and then determined. The worst part was what came next. Instead of actually building something, I would spend an hour or two that evening making a plan. I would sketch out what I was going to do, put together a rough schedule, and sometimes clean up the garage and rearrange things so it felt like I was getting ready to work. It felt productive, but I was not building anything. I would tell myself I just needed my schedule to clear up, and I would get on it, but then something else would grab my attention, and before I knew it, a month had passed and nothing was done. I would pull into the garage, see the same unfinished projects in the rearview mirror, get frustrated, get determined, and start the whole process over again.

One day I stopped planning and started doing. But first, I made myself one rule: no more wood, no more materials, no more supplies until I finished one thing. Not started. Finished. I would not even watch woodworking videos until something was done. That rule changed everything. It closed the door on the next shiny idea and pointed me back at what was already sitting right in front of me. I picked up the project that had been there the longest and did one small thing on it. Not the whole project. Not even a long session. Just one thing. Maybe I sanded one section. Maybe I cut one piece, and the next day I did one more thing, and that often led me to working on the project for a couple of hours. I actually did enjoy the wood projects I was working on.  The projects did not become perfect. They became finished. I built an epoxy table and put plants on it. The cornhole boards were completed and used at summer parties. Wooden rustic flags were finished and sold online or donated at charity events. That is what moving forward looked like in that moment: one rule, one project, one step at a time, without waiting for the right conditions or the right level of readiness. I did not pretend the years of abandoned projects never happened. I knew the history. I just stopped living inside it. I took what those failed starts had taught me and put that knowledge to work instead of letting it sit in the corner collecting dust alongside the projects themselves. And yes, I made mistakes along the way, but I learned from those mistakes, and my woodworking skill got better because of them.

The second story is the opposite. I let something go. For years, I had carried the idea that I should finish my college degree. It was part of the life I thought I was supposed to build. You may recall from Chapter 1 the script that was not quite mine. The degree was on that script. I had the credits. I had the time, or I could have made the time, but I finally asked myself whose life I was building. I realized I had the knowledge, the strength, and the ability to learn what I needed to learn without the degree. I had been working in a different field from my degree, and I was learning far more than I ever would have in a classroom. I did not need the piece of paper to do what I wanted to do. So I released it. I stopped carrying it. The relief was real, and I gained back time and energy I had been spending on longing, guilt, and obligation. Moving forward sometimes means finishing a project. It can also mean releasing what is no longer serving you. I did not erase the history of that degree. I did not pretend I had never started it or that the years of carrying it had not happened. What I learned in those years of working in the field, the knowledge I had built without a classroom, was real and it was mine. I kept the learning. I let go of the obligation. That degree was someone else's idea of what I should have been. Letting it go was moving forward with my own life.

So we come back to you. What would moving forward look like for you this week? Not in general. Not someday. This week. Is there one half-finished project in your garage, your basement, your bookshelf, or the corner of some room that you could take one step on? Is there one thing you have been carrying, looking at in the rearview mirror, that you could turn from "what it was supposed to be" into "what do I do next"? And what did that stalled thing teach you? You are allowed to keep the lesson. You are not required to keep the weight. When you think about your life right now, where does your mind go? Back, or ahead? Sit with that, and then choose one small way to move forward.

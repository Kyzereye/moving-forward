# Chapter 1: Opening — The Rearview Mirror Trap

*Moving Forward: Stop Looking in the Rear View Mirror*

---

When I was young, I had a dream. I was in a car, traveling very fast across desert salt flats. The vehicle was just a chassis, wheels, and a bench seat with a large engine in the front. But there was no body. There were no fenders, no dashboard, and no rearview mirror. I was enjoying the speed and the wind in my hair. At some point I realized no one was driving, but there was someone else at the front of the vehicle working feverishly. I knew right away who it was. He had longish hair blowing in the wind and motorcycle goggles. It was me. I was building my own vehicle to move through this life. I was building my own future.

Did you ever have a dream like this? One so real you could feel it, but when you tried to explain it, the words didn't fit. Maybe it is something you look back on now and see how it defined your life. Or something you wish you had heeded the message. Maybe it wasn't a sleeping dream at all. Maybe you found yourself staring into nothing, and a question surfaced. Or several:

- Why am I following the life I'm building?
- Whose life is this? Mine, my parents', or society's?
- Am I the one driving, or just along for the ride?
- How did I get on this road?
- Do I still know what I wanted before everyone else's wants took over?
- When did I last ask myself what I actually want, without "should" or "supposed to"?
- Am I building a life I chose, or one that chose me?
- Who decided what "success" or "the good life" looks like for me?
- If I could start the blueprint again, would I draw the same one?

It's a lot. It can get heavy. We'll come back to it. For now, sit with the one question that won't let go.

I didn't grow up with a hard life. There was no abusive family situation, at least not by the standards of the time. No drug issues, no serious run-ins with the law. There was no rock bottom. There was no moment when I had to change my thinking or die. I grew up on a farm in rural New York. I had chores. I played outside until the light faded or someone called me in. We had one phone on the wall, one TV, and family meals at the table. School was just there. There was nothing to escape from and nothing to prove. We gathered earthworms that we used the next day to catch the fish we had for dinner that evening. I was bored sometimes. I had friends. The "adults" would play tricks on us and have us running around the property looking for some imaginary thing that they were able to get us to believe was real. I got into the kind of small trouble that doesn't leave a mark. My story is probably pretty average for a kid coming of age in the mid-to-late seventies and early eighties.

I've read plenty of stories where the main character hits a crisis and makes a life-changing decision. Mine wasn't like that. The pressure I felt later wasn't from surviving a bad start; it was from the gap between the life I thought I was supposed to be building and a direction I didn't know existed. So I kept looking back at the script I'd left and at what I thought I was supposed to be. I did not look ahead. There was no dramatic before-and-after. There was just the slow habit of looking back.

I held on to that. I knew I was the creator of my own destiny. I knew the life of my parents was not for me. And yet.

My thirties and forties were filled with attempts to live up to the dream. The other dream. The one that looked like a wife, kids, a big house, the perfect job. I was trying to be who I was and what I thought I was supposed to be. I wasn't down and out. I lived near a large farm on property with a stream. My father and I had our issues, but growing up was not hard. I was in the tension. I never really focused on either one: not fully in the script I was "supposed" to live, not fully in the vehicle I was building. I kept looking in the rearview mirror. That was where I had left that life behind. Or so I thought. But looking back at what I'd left kept me from being fully in the driver's seat of the life I was making.

The mirror became the trap. I remember a time when I first became aware of it. I had just bought a house, broken up with Julie, and been laid off from my nice position with Microsoft. I was in my mid- to late 30s. I caught glimpses of the trap a couple more times over the next 20 years, but it wasn't until my late 50s that I actually took action. It can alter your life: the choices you make, the relationships you're in, the years you spend half-present, the version of yourself you think you're supposed to be. We'll delve into that in a later chapter. We'll look at how the rearview mirror trap works and what it costs. Here at the start, the point is simple. This book is about how to stop looking in it, and keep moving forward.

Also important to this story is looking in the rearview mirror at the life you *had*. We often view the past with rose-colored glasses. It's not as great as you "remember." Yes, there are probably parts of your life you would relive 100 times. But those times also had loss and heartache. The mirror smooths that out. Sometimes going back feels good, even nice. But you have already been there. You have learned the lessons and had the adventure. It is time to breathe and move forward. You can't go back. But you can go forward.

This book is for you if you didn't hit rock bottom but still feel stuck. Maybe you know you're building something of your own but keep glancing back at the life you were "supposed" to want. Or the life you used to have. You can't go back to that life; we'll open that up in a moment.


Maybe you don't have a dramatic story. You just have the sense that you're not quite in the driver's seat, or that you've been half-present for years. In the chapters ahead we'll look at why we get stuck looking back, what moving forward actually means, and how to make it a habit. You'll get practical ways to catch yourself when you're in the mirror, to redirect your focus to the road ahead, and to take the next step instead of replaying the last one. It is not a formula. It is just a way to live with your eyes on where you're going, not on where you have been.

I hope that this information, this story, and what I've learned from my own life will help you get moving forward again. I hope it helps you not miss out on another 10 or 20 years. I hope it helps make whatever years you have left more rewarding.
